Crumbrella · Lifecycle email concept

A gift the buyer never opens

The Period Box has a quirk most email programmes get wrong: the person who pays for it and the person who opens it are two different people, with two different relationships to the brand. This is the email programme I would build to launch and run it.

01

Context

Crumbrella is a confectionery brand founded in Bengaluru in 2021 by my wife Varsha and her friends. I named it and ran its marketing and Instagram. One of the products I created for it was the Period Box: a parcel a boyfriend sends his girlfriend during her period. Inside are fresh brownies and muffins, a soft mini hot-water bag, a little soft toy to hold, and a card the sender writes himself.

It is a sweet idea with a commercial edge. Period care in India is now a real category, from Carmesi and Azah on the product side to a shelf of period gift boxes already on Amazon and Etsy, most filled with roll-ons, wipes and a generic card. What none of them quite own is Crumbrella's specific, tender thing: not a self-care box you buy yourself, but something one person sends another, baked fresh, timed for the few days nobody else markets to.

This piece is the email side of that idea. Crumbrella has not run this programme. It is what I would build, and it is the part of the job I find most interesting, because the product breaks a rule most email setups quietly depend on.

02

The problem

Almost every ecommerce email flow assumes one thing: the person who buys is the person who uses. Welcome the buyer, win back the buyer, recommend the buyer something else. With a gift, that falls apart. The boyfriend pays. The girlfriend opens the box. They want completely different things from Crumbrella, and a single mailing list treats them as one anonymous customer.

There is a second problem underneath it: Crumbrella has no list of Period Box buyers to email in the first place. And a third: a period is private, cyclical and roughly monthly, while a gift is occasional and emotional. A blunt "20% off this weekend" blast is the wrong instrument for any of it.

One purchase. Two people. Two relationships. And a list that doesn't exist yet.
03

Insight

The giver and the recipient are not buying the same thing, so they should not get the same emails.

The boyfriend is buying reassurance. He wants to show up for someone he cares about, often from another city, and have it land well. His emails should make him feel like he did a thoughtful thing, and make it easy to do again. The girlfriend is the one who actually meets the brand: the brownies, the warmth, the card. Hers is the emotional relationship, and she is the one most likely to become a repeat Crumbrella customer in her own right.

That second point is where the money is. Repeat customers drive about 44% of ecommerce revenue while making up around a fifth of the base, and they spend roughly two-thirds more than first-timers (Branvas, 2026). There is a neat tension here too: women are the natural buyers of an artisanal bakehouse like Crumbrella, the audience the brand already has, yet the Period Box buyer is the boyfriend. The product is sold to the person the brand reaches least. That is the whole challenge, and the whole opportunity.

And there is the cycle. A period comes round every month, which makes the Period Box one of the few gifts with a built-in, predictable reason to reorder, with consent.

04

Strategy

Two halves: first build the list with paid media, then run it with a lifecycle email programme split in two.

Building the list (because there isn't one)

No buyer list exists, so paid lead-gen creates it. The key call: frame the ads as a gifting product, not a period or health one. In early 2025 Meta made health and wellness a sensitive category, which blocks period-related interest targeting and strips out lower-funnel optimisation like "Purchase" (Meta health restrictions). Framing the box as a gesture keeps us out of that bucket and is truer to what it is.

  • Meta and Instagram build the mostly-female audience and list: interests across artisanal bakeries, online gifting, chocolate and small-batch brands; lookalikes off Instagram engagers and the growing list; women 18 to 34 at the core, a separate men 20 to 35 set for the direct gifting angle, Bengaluru first.
  • Google and YouTube catch intent, often the boyfriend mid-search: in-market Gifts & Occasions, a custom segment built from gifting keywords and competitor boxes, and a small Search line on terms like "gift for girlfriend on her period" and "long distance relationship gift".
  • The hook: a lead form and landing page offering a free customised card on the first box, in exchange for an email.

Then the lifecycle programme runs every flow on two tracks from a single order: a Giver track and a Recipient track.

Giver track (the boyfriend)
1 · Launch email (the campaign that acquires him)
2 · Order confirmation, reassure + offer a reminder
4 · Reorder reminder, ~4 weeks, opt-in only
Win-back, if he goes quiet
Recipient track (the girlfriend)
Box and card arrive, she scans and opts in
3 · Recipient welcome, "someone sent you this"
Meet the range, she becomes a customer
Her own Crumbrella lifecycle

Goals, each with a number to hit

  • Convert the first-time giver: welcome and confirmation flow. Target 45%+ open, 8%+ conversion, the line below which Klaviyo says welcome flows leak first-purchase revenue.
  • Recover the hesitant giver: a three-step abandoned-cart flow, target 8 to 12% recovery, past the 5% mark of a weak single-email setup.
  • Convert the recipient into her own customer through a consented welcome.
  • Build a reorder habit with an opt-in monthly reminder, handled with restraint.

The flows are where the value sits: across ecommerce they generate about 41% of email revenue from 5.3% of sends, roughly 18 times the revenue per email of a campaign (Klaviyo, 2025). The Recipient track only fires if she opts in via the card, and the reorder reminder only if the giver asks for it. Under India's DPDP Act, period and gifting data is exactly the kind you handle with care, so it is easy to switch off and never assumed.

05

Execution

Four emails carry the idea, and they look different depending on who is reading. The giver's are grounded and chocolate-dark; hers is the emotional beat, lighter and warmer, on cream. The photography does the same work, the same box shot dark for him and soft for her.
