Decoding a Regional Palate

My part in a qualitative study for a new Assamese spice blend: helping run the fieldwork, mine thirty hours of in-home interviews, and frame the consumer insights that shaped the brand's packaging decisions.

Client: a leading Indian spice brand (confidential), through Drshti Strategic Research Services.
Method: qualitative in-home depth interviews with a live cooking module.
Note: confidential engagement, so the client is unnamed and this piece shows the method and my part in it, not the findings.
Skills demonstrated

01

Context

A leading Indian spice brand, long in Eastern Indian kitchens and built on region-specific blends, was moving further into the Northeast with masalas made one dish at a time. It had developed a new blend for Maasor Tenga, the everyday Assamese sour fish curry, and needed to know whether the idea, and the blend itself, would hold up with the women who actually cook it. The brand commissioned Drshti Strategic Research Services to run a qualitative evaluation in Guwahati: ten in-home depth interviews, each up to three hours, each with a live cook-along. I worked on the study as the market research intern, from screening the respondents at the front end to helping shape the recommendation at the back.

02

The problem

Maasor Tenga is not a "masala" dish. Its sourness comes from a fresh agent, tomato, lemon, or a regional fruit called Thekera, and it is cooked by feel. A packaged masala offers convenience to a dish whose appeal is that it is made fresh, so a hall full of strangers asked "would you buy this?" gives you a polite yes and nothing true. That is why the senior team designed the study to go into the kitchen and watch her cook.

That design only pays off if two things hold: the right women in front of the camera, and the discipline to turn three hours of kitchen talk into something a brand can act on. Recruit one person who doesn't really cook the dish and the read is quietly corrupted; stop at "people liked it" and the brand learns nothing it can use. Both ends, the integrity of the inputs and the sharpness of the output, were where my work sat.

03

Screening the room

The respondents came from a recruitment vendor, and a vendor is paid to deliver bodies, not necessarily the right ones. Before anyone was confirmed, I ran backcheck calls: a second, independent screening of each person. I checked that she genuinely cooked or ate Maasor Tenga the way her segment required, that her affinity to the cuisine was real rather than coached for the fee, that there was no hidden bias or rehearsed answer, and that the profile hadn't been padded to clear the screener. Anyone who didn't hold up was flagged and dropped. It is unglamorous work, and it is the whole difference between ten real homemakers and ten people performing for a payout.

04

Guarding the fieldwork

The interviews ran in respondents' homes, streamed live, and I monitored them over video as they happened. My job was to make sure every predecided question got asked and every stage was actually carried out, the dry blend, the cooking, the just-cooked dish, the tasting and the concept reactions, in order and in full, while a trained moderator led in the room. I logged the audio and video evidence and kept running notes through each session, a first read of what was being said while it was still fresh. When something was missed or worth pushing on, it could be caught while the session was still live, not discovered weeks later in a transcript.

05

From transcripts to themes

Once the fieldwork was done, the job was turning roughly thirty hours of footage and transcripts into something the senior team could build on. I built the data layout and the content analysis document, sorting raw verbatims into structured categories, drew a trend analysis across the interviews, grouping consumers by their choices and by the themes that kept surfacing, and wrote topline summaries so the team had a clear early read. To speed that first pass, I brought NotebookLM, an AI tool, into the workflow, using it to sort and tag notes, summarise transcripts and surface early themes for us to test by hand. It cut the slow, manual part of the analysis without taking the judgement out of it.

Clean inputs in, a read the brand could trust out.
06

Framing the recommendation

From there I helped derive and frame the insights and built sections of the final report. Two of the packaging recommendations were mine to surface. One was a naming cue: the dish name alone on the pack wasn't carrying the sour, tangy imagery the curry is known for, so the pack needed to say more to feel right. The other was a colour cue: the image of the cooked dish on the pack had to match how a real cook pictures it, not a more generic, conventionally appetising version, or it read as inauthentic to the exact people the brand was selling to. Small, specific fixes, each drawn straight from what women said in their own kitchens.

07

What my work delivered

None of this is the headline on a chart, and that is the point. By the time the senior team finalised the recommendation, they were working from ten respondents who genuinely belonged in the study, fieldwork that had followed the guide to the letter, a first-level structure that turned thirty hours of kitchen footage into sorted, themed inputs, and a set of specific, usable fixes to the pack.

In a regional launch where getting the authenticity wrong is the fastest way to lose the market, both halves matter: inputs the brand could trust, and recommendations it could act on. The findings stay with the client; what I can show is how a study like this gets run, mined and turned into decisions a brand can use.
